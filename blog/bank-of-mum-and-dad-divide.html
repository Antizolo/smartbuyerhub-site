The Bank of Mum and Dad: how a $35 billion 'lender' split first home buyers into two classes

By RyanUpdated 22 June 20266 min read

There's a viral version of the Australian dream doing the rounds right now: a young couple buying a $1.1 million home with a 5% deposit - not because they out-saved anyone, but because their parents quietly backed the loan. For one half of a generation, that's the story of how they got in. For the other half, it's the reason the gap keeps widening.

Parental help has stopped being a "nice to have". It has become the single biggest predictor of whether a young Australian owns a home - and it has quietly split first home buyers into two classes: those with a Bank of Mum and Dad, and those without.

Australia's ninth-biggest "lender" isn't a bank

If you tallied it all up and dropped it onto the banking league table, the "Bank of Mum and Dad" would sit somewhere around the ninth-largest mortgage lender in the country. Digital Finance Analytics estimates families channel roughly $35 billion into home purchases every year. More than 60% of first home buyers now receive some form of family help, with the average leg-up around $33,000 - and considerably more in the expensive capitals.

This isn't a fringe phenomenon anymore. It's a structural feature of how property changes hands in Australia: an informal lender bigger than most of the actual banks, operating entirely off the books.

The divide nobody voted for

Here's why it matters. Home ownership among Australians aged 25 to 34 has fallen from more than 50% to just 37% in two decades. Over the same stretch, the time it takes a median-income household to save a 20% deposit has climbed from 9 years to 11.2 years. When the finish line moves faster than you can run, who your parents are starts to matter more than what you earn.

The research is blunt about the consequences. Receiving a family transfer of even $5,000 or more makes a young person more than twice as likely to become a home owner. And because wealthier families tend to give more often and give larger amounts, the help doesn't only separate one generation from the last - it widens the gap within a generation, between peers who earn the same salary but start from very different places.

The uncomfortable truth: in today's market, a deposit gifted at 28 can be worth more than a decade of disciplined saving - because it gets you in before the next leg of price growth, not after it.

Two ways parents help - and why one is far smarter

If you're fortunate enough to have family willing and able to help, how they help matters enormously. There are two main routes, and they are not equal.

1. The cash gift

The obvious one: parents hand over a lump sum for the deposit. It works, but it carries real downsides. It can drain the savings your parents may need for their own retirement, and for pensioner parents it can trip Centrelink's gifting rules and reduce their pension. Once the money is gone, it's gone - and it's often more than the household can comfortably spare.

2. The guarantor loan

The structured alternative - and frequently the smarter one - is a guarantor loan. Instead of handing over cash, your parents use the equity in their own home as additional security for part of your loan. No money changes hands. That extra security lifts your effective deposit to the equivalent of 20%, which means you can often avoid Lenders Mortgage Insurance entirely - a saving that typically runs from $15,000 to $35,000 or more on a city purchase.

A quick example: on a $750,000 purchase with a $75,000 (10%) deposit, your parents guarantee roughly the $75,000 gap needed to reach 20%. In the lender's eyes your loan-to-value ratio drops to 80%, the LMI bill disappears, and - because no cash was actually gifted - Centrelink's gifting rules generally aren't triggered. Their savings stay intact; their equity simply does the heavy lifting for a while.

How to do a guarantor loan properly

A guarantor loan is powerful, but it puts your parents' home on the line if things go badly wrong. Done well, that risk is contained and temporary. Three rules matter most:

  • Use a limited guarantee. Cap the guarantee to the shortfall - the deposit gap plus a small buffer - not the entire loan. This single decision is the most important protection your parents have.
  • Build in an exit from day one. The guarantee can be released once your loan falls to around 80% of the property's value, through a mix of repayments and capital growth. For many buyers that's a few years away, not a life sentence.
  • Get independent legal and financial advice. Your parents should understand exactly what they are signing, advised separately from you. A good broker structures the loan so release is realistic - and the family relationship survives it.
The short version: A cash gift is simple but costs your parents their savings and can dent their pension. A guarantor loan keeps their cash intact, can wipe out $15,000-$35,000 of LMI, and is removable once you build enough equity - provided it's a limited guarantee with a clear exit plan.

No Bank of Mum and Dad? You still have a path

If family help simply isn't on the table, you are not locked out - you just need a different door. The federal First Home Guarantee (the 5% Deposit Scheme) now runs with no income caps and no LMI, and is getting buyers in with a median deposit of around $29,000. It won't suit everyone, and it doesn't change the price you pay, but it is the closest thing to a government-issued guarantor - and for buyers without wealthy parents, it can level a field that has tilted hard against them.

What to actually do next

Whichever class you find yourself in, start with the numbers, not the suburb. Work out what you can genuinely borrow and repay using our borrowing power estimator, then map that against your real deposit position - gift, guarantor, or scheme. If your parents are open to helping, get advice before anyone signs anything: the difference between a cash gift and a properly limited guarantor loan can be tens of thousands of dollars, and years of your parents' peace of mind. The dream isn't dead - but in 2026, the smart buyers are the ones who structure the help rather than simply hope for it.
